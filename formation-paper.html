2. Prior Contributions and Differentiation

Chladni's plate experiments (1787) produced the first visual evidence that vibration generates organised geometric structure at nodal points. His figures — symmetric, scale-dependent, reproducible — established that frequency determines geometry, that a passive medium migrates to zero-displacement nodes, and that complexity of formation increases with frequency.

Chladni's contribution: The observation that formation occurs at nodal zero points in acoustic systems.

Differentiation: Chladni identified the phenomenon in a single physical medium (metal plate, sand, acoustic bow). He did not propose a cross-scale law. This paper extends his observation to eight physical scales and identifies the nodal zero point as the universal formation location across all frequency-carrying media.

2.2 John Ernst Worrell Keely (1837–1898) — Sympathetic Vibrational Systems

Keely's experimental work on sympathetic resonance (1872–1898) demonstrated that mechanical systems could be set in motion through vibrational correspondence rather than direct mechanical contact. His central claim — that every material body possesses a fundamental resonant frequency and responds to signals at that frequency regardless of distance — was not formalised in his lifetime.

Keely's contribution: The principle that resonance is sympathetic — that a receiver responds to a transmitter at matching frequency, across a medium, without direct contact.

Differentiation: Keely's documentation was incomplete and his mechanisms disputed. He did not produce a cross-scale unified law. This paper formalises his sympathetic resonance principle as a specific case of the Circumference Law operating at mechanical and cardiac scales.

2.3 Hans Jenny (1904–1972) — Cymatics: The Study of Wave Phenomena

Jenny's two-volume Cymatics (1967, 1974) extended Chladni's method to liquids, pastes, and three-dimensional media. Using a tonoscope, Jenny produced formations in sand, water, iron filings, and biological tissue, establishing that formation geometry is frequency-specific, reproducible, and present in every responsive medium.

Jenny's contribution: Empirical demonstration that Chladni formations are not limited to flat plates or acoustic systems — they are a property of frequency interacting with any responsive medium.

Differentiation: Jenny documented the phenomenon across more media but did not propose a universal law, measure formation thresholds, or describe a formation constant. This paper uses his empirical foundation and extends it to a formal principle with a measurable constant.

2.4 HeartMath Institute (est. 1991) — Cardiac Electromagnetic Field Geometry

Research at the HeartMath Institute (McCraty et al., 1995–2015) established that the human heart generates an electromagnetic field measurable at 3–4 feet beyond the body surface, with toroidal geometry and field density concentrated at the boundary layer of the torus, not at the cardiac centre.

HeartMath's contribution: Measurement of the cardiac electromagnetic field and its toroidal structure. Confirmation that biological organs generate coherent frequency fields extending beyond the physical boundary of the organ.
